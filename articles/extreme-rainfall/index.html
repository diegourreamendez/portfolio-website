The fundamental assumption that has guided water management for a century is failing. "Stationarity"—the idea that natural systems fluctuate within an unchanging envelope of variability—is dead. In the Iberian Peninsula, this loss of predictability is creating a dangerous gap in flood risk management.

For decades, hydrological engineers across Spain relied on historical records to define the "100-year flood." We assumed that while rainfall is chaotic, its statistical properties remain constant over time. However, our latest analysis of the 2020-2025 Mediterranean rainfall cycles reveals a systematic shift. The envelope is not just moving; it is expanding.

Why Stationary Models are Failing

Traditional models fail because they cannot account for the accelerated thermal feedback loops in the Western Mediterranean. As surface sea temperatures rise, the atmospheric capacity for water vapor increases non-linearly. What was once a statistical outlier is becoming a seasonal baseline.

The Autoregressive Revolution

To counter the death of stationarity, we propose a shift to Dynamic Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (D-ARIMA) models. Unlike their static predecessors, these models treat the parameters themselves as time-dependent variables. 

# Hydrology_OS Model Snippet
import h_os_engine as hos

model = hos.NonStationaryARIMA(
    data=rainfall_series,
    drift=True,
    stochastic_volatility=True
)

# Execute 50-year stochastic simulation
results = model.simulate(n_steps=600, ensemble_size=1000)

Impact for Flood Risk Policy

What does this mean for the person on the ground? It means that our current civil engineering standards—the height of sea walls, the diameter of storm drains, the zoning of residential areas—are based on a world that no longer exists. Policymakers must adopt 'Adaptive Infrastructure' frameworks that allow for iterative upgrades as our non-stationary data matures.
